What Storm Damage Looks Like — And What to Do First

⇧ NEW SECTION — Added based on competitor research

After a severe storm passes, damage isn't always visible from the ground. Hail can bruise asphalt shingles without dislodging them. Wind can lift ridge caps and tear step flashing. Water can seep through a one-inch breach and trigger mold growth within 24 to 48 hours.

Your first 72 hours matter most for your insurance claim. Here's what to do immediately:

  • Do not climb on your roof — it may be slippery or structurally compromised
  • Walk your attic and look for daylight, water stains, or damp insulation
  • Document everything with timestamped photos and video before making any repairs
  • Make emergency temporary repairs — tarping, board-ups — to prevent further damage (your policy requires this)
  • Do not discard any damaged materials until your insurance adjuster has inspected them
  • Call your insurance company to open the claim, then call a licensed contractor for an independent inspection before the adjuster arrives

Texas Storm Types: What's Covered and What Isn't

⇧ NEW SECTION — Added based on competitor research

Texas sits at the intersection of hail alley and tornado alley, with a Gulf Coast exposed to hurricanes and tropical storms. It's one of the most storm-prone states in the nation — and homeowners here face insurance coverage rules that differ significantly from other states. Understanding what your policy actually covers is the difference between a full payout and a denied claim.

Hail Damage

Texas averages over 400 hailstorms per year. Hail damage to roofs, HVAC condenser units, gutters, and siding is typically covered under standard homeowners insurance — but your wind/hail deductible applies first. Most Texas homeowners carry a separate wind and hail deductible of 1%–3% of their home's insured replacement cost. On a $300,000 home, that means $3,000–$9,000 out of pocket before your insurer pays anything.

Wind Damage

Straight-line winds, microbursts, and tornadoes cause billions in Texas property damage every year. Most homeowners policies cover wind damage, but coastal Texas homeowners may need a separate windstorm policy through the Texas Windstorm Insurance Association (TWIA). If you're in a TWIA-eligible county along the Gulf Coast, your standard policy likely excludes wind coverage entirely — a gap many homeowners don't discover until after a hurricane.

Water and Flood Damage

Rainwater entering through a storm-damaged roof or broken window is typically covered under your homeowners policy. But flooding from rising water — even during a hurricane — is not. Flood damage requires a separate policy through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP). This is one of the most costly misunderstandings Texas homeowners face after major storms. If you don't have flood insurance and your home floods, your homeowners claim will be denied.

HVAC Storm Damage

Hail and storm debris can severely damage outdoor HVAC condenser units. This is commonly covered under your dwelling or other-structures coverage — but many homeowners never file a claim because they don't think to inspect their outdoor unit after a storm. Emergency HVAC repair and system replacement may be fully covered if the damage is storm-related and documented promptly.

Understanding Your Texas Homeowners Insurance Claim

⇧ EXPANDED — From 4 steps to full 7-step claims walkthrough

The decisions you make in the first 72 hours after a storm can determine whether your insurance claim is fully paid, partially paid, or denied. Here's how the process works in Texas:

  1. File promptly. Texas law requires insurers to acknowledge your claim within 15 days. Most policies require you to notify your insurer within a specific window — sometimes within days of discovering damage. Don't delay.
  2. Get a contractor inspection before the adjuster arrives. Have a licensed Texas contractor document your damage in writing first. This creates an independent baseline to compare against the insurer's estimate. Adjusters frequently undercount damage to gutters, fascia, siding, and HVAC.
  3. Prepare for the adjuster visit. The insurance adjuster works for the insurer — not for you. Walk them through every damaged area yourself. Document their name and direct contact. Ask them to itemize each damage line.
  4. Understand ACV vs. RCV. Your settlement depends on your policy type. Actual Cash Value (ACV) policies subtract depreciation before paying — an older roof gets you far less than replacement cost. Replacement Cost Value (RCV) policies pay what it costs to replace with new materials, minus your deductible only. Review your declarations page to see which you have.
  5. Review the settlement carefully. Compare the adjuster's estimate line-by-line with your contractor's assessment. If the numbers diverge significantly, you have options before accepting.
  6. File a supplemental claim if needed. If your adjuster missed damage or repair costs have risen since the original estimate, you have the right to file a supplemental claim. This is common, legitimate, and often results in additional payment.
  7. Dispute the outcome if underpaid or denied. You can request a re-inspection, invoke the appraisal clause in your policy (a neutral umpire resolves the dispute without litigation), hire a public adjuster to negotiate on your behalf, or file a complaint with the Texas Department of Insurance at 800-252-3439 or tdi.texas.gov.

How to Choose a Contractor After Storm Damage in Texas

⇧ NEW SECTION — Added based on competitor research

After major storms, Texas neighborhoods fill up with out-of-town storm chaser contractors — crews who flood the area for a few weeks and disappear before warranty claims arise. Texas law requires roofing contractors to be licensed by the Texas Department of Licensing and Regulation (TDLR). Verify any contractor's license at license.tdlr.texas.gov before signing anything.

Red flags that signal an untrustworthy contractor:

  • Asking you to sign an Assignment of Benefits (AOB) before your claim is filed
  • Offering to waive or rebate your deductible — this is illegal under Texas law
  • No TDLR license number or verifiable local business address
  • High-pressure same-day tactics with no written estimate
  • Cannot provide proof of general liability insurance and workers' compensation

Texas Homeowners Ask

My insurance company is dragging their feet. What can I do?

Under the Texas Insurance Code, your insurer must acknowledge your claim within 15 days of receiving notice and accept or deny it within 15 business days of receiving your completed proof of loss. If they're missing these statutory deadlines, file a complaint with the Texas Department of Insurance at 800-252-3439 or tdi.texas.gov. Violations of these timelines can constitute bad faith and entitle you to additional damages.

My adjuster's estimate is too low. What are my options?

Get a written estimate from a licensed independent contractor. If the gap is significant, you have three paths: hire a public adjuster to negotiate on your behalf (they work on contingency), invoke the appraisal clause in your policy — which brings in a neutral umpire to resolve the dispute without going to court — or file a complaint with the Texas Department of Insurance. Don't accept a low settlement without exhausting these options.

Can a contractor legally waive my deductible?

No. Under Texas law, it is illegal for any contractor or roofer to waive, absorb, or rebate your insurance deductible. Any contractor who offers this is breaking the law. You should not sign with them, and you can report them to TDLR. This protects you from fraudulent claims and inflated invoices.

Does homeowners insurance cover flooding from hurricane rain?

No. Standard homeowners policies do not cover flood damage from rising water — including hurricane storm surge and flash flooding. You need a separate flood insurance policy through the NFIP (FloodSmart.gov) or a private flood insurer. The one exception: rainwater that enters your home through a wind-damaged roof or broken window is typically covered under your homeowners policy, because the wind damage came first.

What's the difference between ACV and RCV policies?

ACV (Actual Cash Value) pays you the current depreciated value of what was damaged — a 10-year-old roof is worth a fraction of a new one, so your payout is significantly reduced. RCV (Replacement Cost Value) pays what it actually costs to replace the damaged materials with new ones of like kind, minus only your deductible. RCV policies pay out more but have higher premiums. Check your declarations page — the policy type dramatically affects your claim outcome.

How do I verify a Texas contractor's license?

Go to license.tdlr.texas.gov and search by company name or license number. Texas requires roofing contractors to hold an active TDLR license. Electricians, HVAC technicians, and plumbers also require state licensure through separate licensing boards. Verifying licensure before signing any contract protects you from storm chasers and unlicensed crews who may disappear after taking a deposit.
